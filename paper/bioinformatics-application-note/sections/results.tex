\section{Benchmark evidence}

All comparisons are drawn from tracked benchmark reports that record the exact
commands, competitor versions, and correctness checks used (Table~1). VariantFlow
reports a speedup only for an operation whose output has first been verified
against a reference tool; operations that trail a competitor are treated as
optimisation targets rather than claims. The benchmark system configuration is
given in Supplementary Table~S2 and the complete per-workflow timings and
memory measurements in Supplementary Table~S3.

On the DDBJ CHM13 chromosome~22 cohort (3\,715 samples), filters over
FORMAT-rich predicates produced the same records as bcftools while running
4.74--17.78x faster. Query-aware indexing with the \texttt{.vfi} sidecar gave
the largest gains where most blocks can be excluded in advance. A
$\mathtt{FILTER}=\mathtt{PASS}$ predicate, for which block metadata proves that
no record in a skipped block can pass, ran 123--273x faster, and a block-level
allele-frequency threshold predicate ran 4.70--4.93x faster. When few blocks can
be excluded, VariantFlow falls back to a sequential scan and its advantage is
correspondingly smaller.

Population-genetic summaries on the full 1000 Genomes 3\,202-sample
high-coverage cohort (International Genome Sample Resource) confirm that
selective execution scales to whole-chromosome data. Missingness computation ran
4.78x faster than VCFtools on chromosome~22 (1,073,369 records) and 3.67x faster
on chromosome~1 (6,468,094 records), at a peak resident memory of 8.5~MB that
was independent of chromosome size. Linkage disequilibrium ($r^{2}$) ran 9.88x
faster on a 925,000-record dataset. Exporting the data once to Parquet and
issuing repeated queries through DuckDB was 1.19--19.20x faster than repeated
scans of the source file, amortised over the query workload.

Correctness was verified for every reported statistic, because the value of a
faster tool depends on its returning the established answer. All
population-genetic outputs were byte-identical to VCFtools~0.1.17 on the same
inputs (Supplementary Table~S1). An independent reimplementation in scikit-allel
\citep{scikitallel} reproduced per-individual missingness and allele frequency
to machine precision, and VariantFlow's site-frequency spectrum matched
scikit-allel's folded and unfolded spectra exactly. Missing data is the regime
in which diversity estimators are most likely to become biased, so it was tested
directly. VariantFlow implements the unbiased per-site estimators of nucleotide
diversity ($\pi$) and divergence ($d_{XY}$) introduced by pixy \citep{pixy2021};
on an all-sites cohort with 15\% of genotypes set to missing, its per-window
$\pi$ and $d_{XY}$ pairwise counts were identical to pixy across every window
(Supplementary Table~S4). The full validation procedure is described in
Supplementary Note~S2.

VariantFlow implements the population-genetic statistics that can be computed in
a single streaming pass over sites or windows: allele frequency, missingness,
heterozygosity, Hardy--Weinberg equilibrium, $\pi$, the missing-data-aware
$\pi$ and $d_{XY}$ estimators, Tajima's $D$, the site-frequency spectrum,
$F_{ST}$, and linkage disequilibrium (Figure~1; estimator definitions in
Supplementary Note~S1). Statistics that require the full genotype or haplotype
matrix to be resident in memory, including selection scans, principal-component
analysis, and $D$- and $f$-statistics, fall outside this streaming scope and are
deferred to matrix-oriented libraries such as scikit-allel; Supplementary
Table~S5 maps the full correspondence.
